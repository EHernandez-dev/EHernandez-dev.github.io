Simulation-Based Inference

Teaching neural networks to read cosmology from galaxy clusters

Galaxy clusters are the largest gravitationally bound structures in the universe, and everything about them, from how their gas glows in X-rays to how they bend the cosmic microwave background, carries information about the cosmology they grew in. The catch: that signal is entangled with messy astrophysics. Feedback from active galactic nuclei, supernovae, and star formation reshapes a cluster from the inside, and disentangling "how the universe works" from "how the cluster's own physics works" is one of the central challenges of precision cosmology.

From cluster profiles to 28 parameters

In Hernández-Martínez et al. (2024), I led a study showing just how much of that entangled information can be recovered. Using the CAMELS-zoomGZ simulation suite, we stacked galaxy cluster profiles of five observable properties: X-ray surface brightness, gas density, temperature, metallicity, and the Compton-y signal. We then trained neural networks to invert them, inferring every single one of the 28 cosmological and astrophysical parameters of the IllustrisTNG model at once.

28 parameters inferred simultaneously from stacked profiles
r ≥ 0.97 correlation for all cosmological parameters, including Ωm, H0, and σ8
S/N 2.5 noise level at which key parameters still remain recoverable

Real observations never hand you a perfect profile, so we stress-tested the approach against reality. Restricting profiles to radial ranges between 0.1 and 0.7 R200c, mimicking what current instruments actually resolve, and injecting up to 40% Gaussian noise (signal-to-noise ratios as low as 2.5) degrades the inference far less than one might fear: key parameters such as Ωm, H0, and the stellar initial mass function slope remain robust even under extreme noise. We also found that full radial profiles consistently beat integrated quantities, which means the shape of a cluster's profile, not just its total signal, is doing real cosmological work.

The CAMELS connection

This work lives inside CAMELS (Cosmology and Astrophysics with MachinE Learning Simulations), a suite of thousands of simulated universes in which cosmology and astrophysics are varied systematically, built precisely so that machine learning models can learn to tell the two apart. Beyond using CAMELS, I contribute to growing it: I run simulations with the Magneticum galaxy formation model, adding an independent physics implementation to the suite so that inference methods like the one above can be tested for robustness across models, not just within one.
